## About the Collection

Regarding [The Gary Project](https://archives.iu.edu/catalog/InU-Ar-VAB8852), this particular project is a hallmark of Richard M. Dorson’s later work resulting in the publishing of his book, Land of the Millrats. This particular project focuses on a city in Indiana and actively  juxtaposes his earlier work (and represents his heart-filled connection to Indiana) that resulted in publications and articles of folklore around the world. This series selection contains project files, newspaper clippings, slides, photocopies of newspaper clippings, audio cassettes, and videotapes. It is my own hope that these selected items within this project work to sufficiently highlight the myriads of varied cultures that find  purchase within Gary, Indiana, as portrayed by Richard Dorson.

Please note that due to copyright restrictions for audio-visual material, links to the Indiana University’s Media Collections Online will be available for specifically audio-visual material. You must be logged in to Media Collections Online in order to properly view and listen to the material. For further information and questions about gaining access to view and listen to the items, please contact Indiana University Collections at [archives@iu.edu](mailto:archives@iu.edu) or by phone at 812-855-1127.

### Recommended Users

Although users of all backgrounds are encouraged to utilize this curated selection to their research and academic benefit, the individuals who may find most use are existing members or affiliates with Indiana University or those interested in the wider Folklore field as an academic discipline. For students looking at this collection, you will be able to sufficiently see how fieldwork was conducted during that time, including the fieldwork notes taken and audio recordings recorded of individuals in the Gary area. For faculty, this collection is an opportunity to see how Dorson was able to capture the life of Gary, Indiana in work that later resulted in his published book. Since Dorson is a prominent academic in the field of folklore, any insight into his work as showcased through this particular digital collection is insightful and welcome.

Many archival boxes with these records have also been digitized and published to Media Collections Online for Indiana University affiliated individuals to view, making this digital collection some of the first public-facing aspects of showcasing some materials that were previously behind staff walls.
